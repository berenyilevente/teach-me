Lesson 1 · The foundation · ~10 minutes

The Blunder-Check Routine

A tiny habit you run on every move so you stop giving pieces away — the single fastest way to gain rating at your level.

Why this first, before openings? At 750–950, games are decided by who hangs fewer pieces, not by who knows more opening theory. Your puzzle skill is probably already higher than your game rating — that gap is blunders and lost focus, and it's completely fixable with a routine. We start here because it pays off in your next game.

The routine — four checks, every move

Most blunders happen because we react to our own plan and forget the board is shared. The fix is a fixed loop you run before you ever touch a piece:

  1. 1Threat?Your opponent just moved — what did it attack? Look at their Checks, Captures, and Threats (CCT). Ask: "What are they trying to do to me?"
  2. 2Is my move safe?Picture the move done. Can that piece be taken for free on its new square? Did moving it stop defending something or open a line?
  3. 3Scan all my pieces.Quick sweep of the whole board: is anything of mine now hanging (attacked and undefended)? Fix that before anything else.
  4. 4My turn to attack.Now look for your CCT — a check, a free capture, a threat that wins material. Don't just be safe; grab what they left hanging.

Remember it as: “What’s the threat — is my move safe — what’s free for me?”

The vocabulary here (hanging, en prise, CCT) lives in your Glossary — keep it open while you play.

Watch the loop run

Position: Your opponent just played a bishop to b4.

1. Threat? That bishop now attacks your knight on c3, which is pinned to your king. CCT flags a real threat. ✔ noticed.

2. Is my move safe? You were about to play Ne5 — but the knight is pinned and can't legally move. Good thing you checked.

3. Scan. Nothing else of yours is hanging.

4. My turn? You can break the pin and develop with a3 or Bd2. Calm, no material lost.

Without the routine, you'd play Ne5 on autopilot, notice it's illegal, grab something else fast — and that rushed mood is exactly when the real blunder slips out.

The focus half — beating autopilot

A routine only works if you actually run it. Three habits make that automatic:

  • Sit on your hands. Don't touch a piece until you've decided and run the check. The physical pause is the whole trick.
  • Play rapid, not bullet, while learning. You can't build a thinking habit in a 3-minute game. Train the routine in 10–15 minute games first; speed comes later.
  • One breath on every opponent move. When they move, breathe once and ask "what's the threat?" before you think about your own idea. Most missed captures are missed because we never looked at their side of the board.

Primary source — watch this

GM Aman Hambleton's “Building Habits” series is the best thing you can watch for this mission: a grandmaster deliberately plays from low ratings using strict, simple rules — don't hang pieces, always check what's attacked, develop, trade when ahead. Watch one episode and notice how often he pauses to ask "what does that move do?" That pause is the routine you just learned.
