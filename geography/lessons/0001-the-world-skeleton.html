Lesson 0001 · Practical World Geography

The World Skeleton

Before any region makes sense, you need the frame it hangs on: 7 continents and 5 oceans.

You told me the continents feel fuzzy. That's the right place to start — because every country you'll ever read about in the news lives inside one of seven continents, touching one of five oceans. Get this skeleton solid and you'll never again hear a place named and have no idea which part of the world it's in. That alone closes most of the gap.

The 7 continents — biggest to smallest

Don't memorise this as a list. Picture a world map and place them. Size order is worth knowing because it tells you where the people and the news are.

Largest → smallest

  1. Asia — the giant. East half of the big landmass. China, India, the Middle East, most of Russia.
  2. Africa — south across the Mediterranean from Europe. Sits on the equator.
  3. North America — top-left of the map. USA, Canada, Mexico.
  4. South America — hangs below North America. Brazil is most of it.
  5. Antarctica — the frozen bottom. No countries, just research bases.
  6. Europe — the crowded north-west corner of the big landmass. "The West."
  7. Australia / Oceania — the smallest. Bottom-right, in the Pacific.

The one idea that ties it together: Asia, Europe and Africa are all one connected landmass — the "Old World." The Americas are a separate strip down the left. Australia and Antarctica float at the bottom. That's the whole planet in one sentence.

The 5 oceans — biggest to smallest

Largest → smallest

  1. Pacific — the huge one between Asia and the Americas.
  2. Atlantic — between the Americas and Europe/Africa (the one you fly across to the US).
  3. Indian — south of Asia, between Africa and Australia.
  4. Southern — the ring of water around Antarctica.
  5. Arctic — the frozen cap around the North Pole.

Cover and recall

Look away from the screen. Out loud, name the 7 continents from biggest to smallest, then the 5 oceans. Stuck on one? That's exactly the gap worth closing — glance back, then try again from the top. Retrieving it from memory is what makes it stick; re-reading does not.
